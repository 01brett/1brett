Russian painter Kazimir Malevich was the progenitor of Suprematism, an abstract art movement characterized by simple geometric shapes and associated with ideas of spiritual purity. The Black Square is considered by Malevich to be his most pure painting.

One color. One shape. One focal point. This painting is a triumph of focus. Contrast with modern enterprise software where the motto is features, features, features. It’s hard to say no, especially in software. Here’s Steve Jobs on the subject:

People think focus means saying yes to the thing you’ve got to focus on. But that’s not what it means at all. It means saying no to the hundred other good ideas that there are. You have to pick carefully. I’m actually as proud of the things we haven’t done as the things I have done. Innovation is saying no to 1,000 things.

Boiling The Ocean

My first UX design job was redesigning the website at the Museum of Flight. The website was a sprawling monstrosity containing a customized page for almost every exhibit and artifact at the museum. The team was me and the webmaster. We had three months, and we had to use Drupal.

The old website was not responsive. It was about 800px wide and didn’t move at all when resizing the browser. Desktop experience was the mobile experience. Current and upcoming events were buried under secondary navigation — and below the fold. Visitor information was hidden on a secondary page. Important basics, such as museum address and ticket prices, were buried below a bunch of text. Someone trying to visit the museum — the main revenue driver — couldn’t glean address, hours, ticket prices without scrolling.

My goals:

  • Make the website responsive
  • Put basic visitor information on homepage
  • Promote events better and more prominently
  • Simplify the donation form
  • Convince Museum staff nav didn’t have to reflect internal departments
  • Modify a free Drupal theme so it was easy to implement
  • Fast loading pages

That’s a lot of goals. And as my first real website project, I didn’t have a sense for how long tasks actually took or the kinds of obstacles I’d encounter. Kind of like leaving for an appointment but not accounting for traffic.

Beautiful Sketches

The project started with the webmaster and me making a sitemap. The website had so many pages. We abandoned a “true” sitemap in favor of a “most important” site map that would cover important pages.

As I’ve done more Information Architecture in my career, I can see this unravelling from a mile away. I don’t think a “greatest hits” focus for the v1 of the website was a bad move, but failing to outline the full size of the website was a mistake.

I immediately jumped into sketches, creating various templates for mobile, tablet, and desktop. I spent too much time on those sketches. Coming from a graphic design background, I thought that every artifact had to be beautiful. I’d spend absurd amounts of time shading drop-shadows on buttons. I didn’t even show the sketches to any of my managers. I was naive to assume their not asking to see was a license to stay in an Ivory Tower.

Showing stuff to stakeholders, even when they don’t ask for it, is a great way to guide management respectfully.

Concurrent with sketching, I started beefing up my front-end prowess with tutorial videos from Team Treehouse. Two things about watching tutorial videos: having baseline knowledge is a huge advantage; video tutorial courses take a lot of time to go through.

I cleaned off the rust and learned a couple new things (Sass and very basic JavaScript). I tried to learn Drupal but couldn’t grok it. That was my first big obstacle to a key goal of easy implementation. Not becoming a PHP expert overnight meant the webmaster needed to beef up his Drupal powers. And since neither of us was fast, our development speed slowed to a crawl.

Designer/Developer

Wearing both hats is hard. With discipline, a designer can develop the design. I ended up bouncing back and forth from sketches to Sketch to coding in the browser. Problems cropped up when trying to get a component pixel perfect before moving on. This is where I struggled to switch back and forth — the finesse. I felt like Jekyll and Hyde.

Trying to speed up the process, I used a front-end framework called Foundation. It’s a lot like Bootstrap. This framework made basic page generation really easy. However, changing the style or structure outside of the allowed areas was a huge time suck. My zeal for visual perfection led to somewhat ridiculous overrides of the default styles. God help me when I changed my mind and had to undo everything.

Prototypes For Everyone

I thought building a prototype in static HTML/CSS would make theming easier. I stuck my prototype online using Github pages. I was shocked how easy it was to use live code to check how stuff rendered in different browsers. Once I pushed an update, the webmaster would take the code and copy it into the Drupal template. This was the theoretical plan. I’m not sure why, but the Foundation Drupal template we used didn’t make it easy for a 1:1 transfer from the Foundation Static website I’d made. I think the Drupal template had very specific page layouts my design didn’t follow. I don’t really know. Software is hard.

As our deadline approached, it was clear we weren’t going to hit our mark. Transferring the static HTML to Drupal was slow going. My boss in marketing really liked the prototype and was anxious to present the final site to the rest of the museum.

I wanted at least one delightful feature designed and developed, so after the blown deadline, I spent a week making a fun, simplified donation form.

Lesson Learned

Focus comes down to two things:

  • Maturity — the ability to respond to the environment in an appropriate manner
  • Experience — the knowledge or mastery of an event or subject gained through involvement in or exposure to it

Lots of talks I’ve heard from senior designers about their careers include humorous recounts of early career mistakes. Stuff like blowing a deadline, promising a client too much, working three days straight cause they promised too much, and losing work when Illustrator or Photoshop crashed. Everyone had to learn these skills, often the hard way.

I don’t think focus is an obvious skill. It feels obvious, yet lots of teams and individuals struggle with it. I think our culture degrades our ability to focus. Perhaps the explosive popularity of yoga makes sense.

The Waterfall Methodology.

An ugly relic of how software used to be made, a past tense in the age of Agile. Yet, Waterfall’s still around, often lurking under the guise of Agile. I think Waterfall comes more naturally than we want to admit.

It’s why collaborative teams are so valued. They aren’t the default.

Greenfield

My first greenfield feature was called Pathways. Checklists, process scripts, and procedures have been around for a long time in medical clinics, historically using paper. Some of the procedures don’t have paper forms. These procedures had to be learned and memorized.

Pathways would solve two problems: robust documentation and “plug and play” procedure. Clinics would enter the 21st with electronic documentation and provide better quality of care across different nurses. The Pathways procedure script could be done by any nurse with a solid minimum of quality care. The procedure script helped fill in the gaps with new and less experienced nurses.

How could I improve a paper form?

Paper forms have too much context. The “If yes, answer questions 3 and 4” kind of questions. Removing unneeded questions would help reduce cognitive load. I wanted the Pathway procedure script to feel focused and tight. Additional questions could populate when needed but not a moment sooner.

Stripe uses a similar approach in their credit card payment dialog.

It has Email, Credit Card number, Expiration Date, CVC Code, and that’s it! If the dialog needs more info, such as a phone number, it asks, but only if it’s needed. Check out what happens when the “Remember me” checkbox is clicked.

Boxes In Boxes

I created a modular design system. Each question became a modular component that could have any variety of nested components inside it. This was to make sure branching logic and nesting would work with extra work. Moving up a level, questions could be inside other modular components for specific groups of questions. My aim was to make it easy to insert special sequences, such as abandon Pathway or initiate Emergency Pathway. The modular layout made for clean delineations of where the form would change.

Initial prototypes were a hit with customers. Everything was going great. I showed the developers. They understood what I was going for but execution would be tough, like really tough.

To their credit, the developer team pulled off the feature by the deadline. But, the tight timeline did result in a lot of tradeoffs on design, both visual and interaction.

Lesson Learned

I sold stakeholders and customers on a design solution that was hard to build. I didn’t set up the developer team for success. They were seen as the blockers to releasing this amazing new flagship feature, not me with my nearly impossible design.

After the release, I talked with the developers about what I could do better next project. They told me I could show them sketches and early comps before everything got sold down the river.

As luck would have it, a month later I had the chance to work with the same developer team on another feature. We were under an insane deadline crunch (even for our company). Management stuck us in a conference room to “hammer it out” together since it would probably be faster (irony!). This time around, we collaborated in realtime on the whiteboard. No Ivory Tower this time. The whole feature was built in less than a week. The previous feature took a month.

Continuing Education

I don’t think this lesson is one that’s learned and done forever. Rather, I think learning gets deeper and richer as time goes on. An initial “Aha!” moment and continued education. Kinda like exercising. Getting in shape, then staying in shape.

When I reflect on work I’ve enjoyed the most, it’s always the collaborative stuff. Working with people is hard, but it’s also what makes work meaningful.

This is what my parents (and others) have assured me having children is like.

The New York Times Magazine wrote an awesome exposé about Google’s search for the perfect team. So, what made a team succeed? Psychological safety. The best teams all looked different, too. Everyone on the team felt heard and respected. Not only is that a good approach for work, but for life.
